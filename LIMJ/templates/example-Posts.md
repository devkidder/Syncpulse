## Post Version A: Personal Profile

I spent 3 years paying a law firm $18K/year for work I can now do with a $20/month Claude subscription.

Most of it wasn't specialized litigation or patent prosecution. It was templating. Redlines. Frameworks. Contract triage.

Contract negotiation used to take 12 days (waiting for lawyer + back-and-forth). Now it takes 3.

Here's what actually changed:

**Month 1**: I documented every contract category I deal with and asked Claude to generate annotated templates. Took 6 hours total.

**Months 2-12**: Every new contract gets pasted into Claude with context. I get a flagging report and a negotiation strategy in 10 minutes. On the rare high-stakes contract, I escalate to actual counsel—but with defined scope, which cuts the bill 80%.

The math: $18K lawyer spend → $240/year Claude → ~$2K my time. Savings: $16K/year.

What I did with that $16K: Hired a contract engineer. Extended runway 6 weeks. Less stress.

Is this replacing specialized legal work? No. Is it replacing 80% of what most founders overpay lawyers for? Absolutely.

Question: Are you paying a law firm for work that could be commoditized? What would you do with those dollars back?

## Post Version B: Company Page (Fused Gaming LLC)

At Fused Gaming, we're watching the legal tech landscape shift in real time.

Until recently, contract work meant: hire a law firm, pay $18K-30K annually, wait 12 days for review, negotiate from incomplete information.

Smart founders are running an experiment: use AI for templating + triage, escalate only the high-stakes work to human counsel.

The results are striking:

**Faster**: 3-day cycle instead of 12
**Cheaper**: $240/year vs. $18K
**Smarter**: AI flags blind spots you'd miss; you negotiate with full information

We've documented this workflow for our portfolio founders. Contract negotiation now takes hours instead of weeks.

The shift: legal work is becoming *tiered*. Routine work gets commoditized (AI handles it). Expert work commands higher rates because the scope is tighter and stakes are real.

For founders: this is a cost lever you control. For law firms: this is a structural problem requiring a repositioning toward higher-value work.

The conversation is happening now. Where do you stand?
